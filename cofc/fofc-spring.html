One of the themes of this course is size or scale. We move up in scale from single programs in Fall quarter to collections of programs (operating systems) and then to collections of computers (networked applications) in Winter Quarter.

Another theme is depth of understanding. A programmer's understanding grows through three stages. In the first stage a programmer works by imitation and trial-and-error, copying and modifying examples. In the second stage a programmer can create an original program and predict its behavior in particular situations, based on an internal mental picture of how the computer executes the constructs of the programming language. In the third stage the programmer can make judgments about the overall correctness of a program, and predict its behavior in a wide variety of situations. In this stage the programmer creates an abstract model of the program and analyzes it by deduction or simulation.

By the end of Winter term we should be well into the second stage. In Spring we will try to enter the third stage. We may experiment with modelling tools like Alloy and Spin.

We may read books on usability such as The Trouble With Computers by Thomas K. Landauer or The Inmates are Running the Asylum by Alan Cooper.
